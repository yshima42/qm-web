Does this sound familiar? Listening to someone's problems, lending them money, dragging yourself to social gatherings you didn't have the energy for. When they say "thank you," you feel a little lighter for a moment. But on the way home, your body feels heavy. You crawl into bed, and your mind won't stop spinning.

Among people struggling with addiction, this pattern of giving too much of yourself is surprisingly common. It's not just kindness. Something deeper is driving the behavior.

There's a perspective that [addiction doesn't start as a "problem" -- it starts as a "solution" to inner pain](/en/blog/addiction-is-solution-not-problem). Self-sacrifice may be another kind of "solution," an attempt to fill that hollow space inside.

## What Lies Behind "I Have to Be Useful"

### Not Knowing Your Own Worth

"I'm nothing special." So many people carry that thought in the back of their minds.

When you can't feel your own value, you pour yourself into helping others so you can hear "thank you" and, just for a fleeting moment, feel like you're allowed to exist. You chase that moment, so you give again. And again. You can't stop.

In psychology, this is called "contingent self-worth." It's a state where you can't accept yourself without validation from others. Research by Crocker & Wolfe (2001) found that the more people depend on external approval for their sense of self-worth, the higher their risk of stress and depression. If you've lived it, that probably doesn't come as a surprise.

### When "I Can't Let Them Dislike Me" Takes Over

"I don't want to be hated." "I don't want to be abandoned." These fears control people's behavior far more than we realize.

You offer yourself up so the other person will need you. But relationships held together that way are fragile. If the other person walks away, you're left with nothing. So you cling even harder.

There's another tricky layer here: the desire for control hiding behind self-sacrifice. You think you're helping, but what you're actually doing is trying to manage the other person's actions and emotions. In clinical settings, this is known as "codependency." The structure of soothing your own anxiety by giving is strikingly similar to addiction itself.

### The Belief That "I Won't Be Loved Unless I Do Something"

Picking up the tab. Lending money. Looking after people who never asked for your help.

Some people genuinely believe they can only be loved by giving. In most cases, this traces back to childhood -- to not receiving unconditional love. As Bowlby's attachment theory shows, people who couldn't form secure attachments in early childhood tend to repeat the same unstable pattern as adults: "I won't be loved unless I do something."

But that isn't love. It's a transaction. And transactions eventually fall apart.

## What's Happening Inside the Brain

The link between self-sacrifice and addiction isn't just emotional. It can be explained by how the brain works, too.

When you help someone and they're grateful, your brain releases dopamine. That rush of elation after volunteering -- the so-called "helper's high." There's nothing wrong with that in itself. The problem is that this feel-good response travels through the **same neural circuits** as gambling or alcohol. The loop of "someone thanks me, it feels good, I want more" gets wired into the same reward-learning system as addiction.

On top of that, self-sacrificing people carry chronic stress. When you constantly push your own needs aside, of course you do. Research by Baumeister et al. (1998) found that continuously suppressing yourself drains your willpower "fuel," and eventually the brakes on your impulses stop working. The result: [wanting to quit but being unable to](/en/blog/why-people-become-addicted) accelerates further and further.

## What's Left After All That Giving

What do you get from devoting yourself to others? A temporary sense of security. A faint feeling of "I was useful."

But what do you lose?

When gratitude doesn't come back, self-loathing floods in. "See, I really am worthless." Once you're caught in the [cycle of blaming yourself too harshly](/en/blog/stop-blaming-yourself), addictive behavior tends to escalate even more.

Relationships that only work because you're giving will warp eventually. You end up fostering dependency in the other person, too. And no matter how much you run around for others trying to fill the emptiness inside, the root problem stays hidden, untouched.

## So What Can You Do?

Helping people isn't inherently bad. But if it's wearing you down, maybe it's worth pausing for a moment.

### Put Your Own Oxygen Mask on First

You've probably seen the airplane safety video. "Please secure your own oxygen mask first." That applies directly to addiction recovery.

"Do I honestly have the capacity for this right now?" "How am I going to feel after I do this?" Asking yourself those questions is the first step. A hand extended while you're gasping for air can't hold anyone up.

### Practicing the Word "No"

You don't have to take on everything. Saying "Sorry, I can't right now" isn't rejecting the other person. It's an act that protects both of you.

Psychologist Henry Cloud writes in his book *Boundaries* that "healthy boundaries aren't rejection -- they exist to protect both sides." Feeling guilty about saying no is a natural reaction. But you don't have to keep obeying that guilt.

### Ask Yourself Why You're Doing This

"I want them to be grateful." "I don't want them to dislike me." If those motives come first, is what you're doing really "help"? Looking honestly at your motives is scary. But facing them is a major step in recovery.

### It's Hard to See Your Own Patterns Alone

Your own patterns are difficult to spot by yourself. Talking to a counselor or a professional can lead to surprising discoveries. [Peer support -- connecting with others who share similar experiences](/en/blog/peer-support) -- often becomes the catalyst for recognizing your own behavioral patterns. Even just reading other people's stories in a community like QuitMate can help. Sometimes, simply realizing "I'm not the only one" is enough to feel a little lighter.

## Taking Care of Yourself Is Not Selfish

Self-sacrificial helping looks beautiful from the outside. But if you're falling apart behind the scenes, it's not making anyone happy.

Fill your own cup first. That's not selfishness -- it's building the foundation you need to truly support the people around you.

If you're feeling right now that "maybe I've been giving too much of myself," the fact that you noticed is already proof you're moving forward.
