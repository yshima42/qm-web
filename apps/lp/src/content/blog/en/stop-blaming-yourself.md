You wake up in the middle of the night and stare at the ceiling.

"I did it again." That regret keeps spinning in the dark room, over and over. You've lost count of how many times. You promised yourself you'd stop. Is your willpower just too weak? Are you a hopeless person?

If that's where you are right now, I'd like you to read just a little further.

## The hardest part isn't the relapse itself

The real pain of addiction often isn't the behavior — it's the self-loathing that follows.

You decided to quit gambling, but the very next day you walked into a pachinko parlor again. You swore off alcohol, but there you were at the convenience store, reaching for a can. And the voice that hits you in that moment: "I knew it — I'm hopeless." "Nothing I do matters anymore."

In a related article, "[What if Addiction Was Never the 'Problem' — But the 'Solution'?](/en/blog/addiction-is-solution-not-problem)," I wrote about how addictive behavior often begins as a form of self-medication for emotional pain. In this article, let's look at another source of suffering — **the way we keep blaming ourselves** — and how it affects recovery.

## When you blame yourself, your brain screams louder for more

This might surprise you, but blaming yourself actually gets in the way of recovery. Reflecting and telling yourself "next time will be different" — that's natural. But when "I'm no good" becomes a constant loop, something tricky starts happening inside your body.

When you keep beating yourself up, stress hormones (cortisol) flood your system. Anxiety rises, and the prefrontal cortex — the part of your brain that acts as the brakes — starts losing its grip. As I touched on in "[Why Do People Become Addicted?](/en/blog/why-people-become-addicted)," fighting urges with weakened brakes is incredibly hard.

In other words, it works like this:

**Blame yourself -> Stress increases -> Harder to resist urges -> You do it again -> Blame yourself even more**

When you're trapped in this loop, it's only natural to feel like "it's because my willpower is weak." But the reality is that the self-blame itself is inviting the next addictive behavior. The U.S. National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) has also pointed out that feelings of shame and stigma are barriers to treatment.

What I want you to remember here is this: addiction is not a weakness of willpower. It's a chronic condition where the brain's reward system has been altered. The reason quitting smoking or gambling is so hard isn't because there's something wrong with you — it's because rewriting the patterns your brain has learned is a process that takes time.

## Breaking down "Why did I do that?"

On the day it happens again, in the middle of that awful feeling, you might think, "I don't even want to think about it." But what if you shifted your perspective just a little?

Try treating "the day I failed" as "the day I spotted one of my patterns."

There's a concept in psychology called **ABC analysis**. The name sounds a bit formal, but what you actually do is simple — just write down "what happened before (A)," "what I did (B)," and "what happened after (C)."

For example, something like this:

> My boss said something harsh at work (A) -> I bought alcohol on the way home (B) -> I felt relieved for a moment, but the next morning I was filled with terrible regret (C)

When you lay it out like that, "why on earth did I do that?" shifts from a vague sense of self-loathing to "oh, that stress was the trigger." It stops being ammunition for self-blame and becomes a clue for being better prepared next time.

A notebook, a note on your phone — anything works. You don't need to write it perfectly at all. As I wrote in "[Simply Reflecting Can Change Your Behavior](/en/blog/reflection-changes-behavior)," just giving yourself a small space for reflection can change how you respond the next time an urge hits.

## From "blaming" to "supporting"

So what can you do instead of blaming yourself?

One thing is **putting yourself in a place where you don't have to blame yourself.** The presence of people who've been through the same thing is bigger than you might think. "Oh, it wasn't just me." That feeling alone can be enough to make your shoulders drop with relief. [Research on peer support](/en/blog/peer-support) has shown that connecting with others going through similar experiences can increase treatment continuation rates by about 1.4 times.

Another is **shifting your focus from "what I couldn't do" to "what I did do."** Not "I couldn't keep it up for three days," but "I made it through one day." Today I almost reached for it, but I held off for ten minutes. Those ten minutes are a big deal. Stacking up small successes like that gradually builds the feeling that "maybe I can do this too."

And the ABC analysis I mentioned earlier — think of it like a journal. The act of keeping a record itself becomes practice in seeing yourself objectively. Try using it not as a tool to blame yourself, but as a tool to understand yourself.

## You are not weak

You decided to quit. You slipped again. And yet you're thinking, "let me try one more time."

Being in that cycle doesn't make you weak. You keep getting back up no matter how many times you fall. That takes real strength.

Tell yourself, just a little: "I've come a long way."
