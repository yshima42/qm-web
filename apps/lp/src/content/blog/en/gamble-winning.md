## You've Probably Heard This at a Party

"Between pachinko and the horses, I'm up overall."

Said with total confidence. You hear the same thing on social media and even in addiction recovery communities. The person isn't lying — at least, not intentionally. In their memory, it really is true.

But how much can you trust that memory?

## A Clear-Eyed Look at How Gambling Actually Works

![Horse Racing](../images/horse-racing.png)

Every form of gambling has what's called a "house edge" — basically, **the cut the house takes**.

For pachinko and slot machines, that's 15-20% of what you put in. For horse racing, about 25%. For lotteries, more than half of the money never comes back. In other words, every time you bet 100 yen, somewhere between 15 and 50 yen is gone before the game even starts.

You can win once or twice on luck alone. But over 100 bets, 1,000 bets, that invisible fee piles up, and your balance steadily drifts into the red. In mathematics, this is called the "law of large numbers" (Tversky & Kahneman, 1971) — the more you play, the closer your results converge to the theoretical average.

This is exactly why the idea that "if I just keep going, I'll win it back" doesn't hold up structurally.

## How Your Brain Creates the Illusion of Winning

And yet, people still feel like they're "up overall." That's not because they lack willpower. It's because of quirks in the brain — what psychologists call **cognitive biases**.

### You Remember Your Wins in Vivid Detail

The day you won 50,000 yen? You can recall what you had for dinner that night. But the day you lost 30,000? "Eh, just an off day" — barely a trace left in your memory.

This is **confirmation bias** — the brain's tendency to latch onto information that supports what you already believe, while filtering out anything inconvenient (Nickerson, 1998). Even if the net result is a 40,000-yen loss, it still feels like you're winning. Memory isn't a ledger. It gets edited to tell the story you want to hear.

### "I've Lost Five Times in a Row, So I'm Due"

If black comes up ten times in a row on the roulette wheel, it's tempting to think, "Surely red is next." But the roulette wheel has no memory. The odds of red on the next spin are exactly the same, no matter how many times black has come up.

This thinking pattern is called the **gambler's fallacy**. That baseless feeling of "it's bound to hit soon" becomes the fuel that keeps people betting.

### "I've Put in Too Much to Stop Now"

After spending 100,000 yen, you think, "If I quit now, that money was all for nothing," and throw in another 50,000. If you think about it rationally, that 100,000 is gone whether you keep playing or not. You know this in your head. But your emotions won't let you hit the brakes.

This is known as the **sunk cost fallacy**. The fear of "cutting your losses" ends up leading to even bigger ones.

As we touched on in [Why Can't You Stop Gambling?](/en/blog/why-cant-you-stop-gambling), these biases are built into the structure of the brain itself — they aren't something that only happens to certain people. That's precisely why recognizing "this is happening to me too" matters.

## The Real Reason People Can't Let Go of "Winning Overall"

![Gambling Reality](../images/gambling-reality.png)

The deeper someone falls into gambling addiction, the more fiercely they tend to insist they're "up overall."

Why?

Because as long as they believe they're winning, they don't have to see their behavior as a problem. "I'm ahead, so it's fine." "The losses are temporary." "I'll make it back next time." These phrases become a shield to protect themselves.

At its core, addiction often starts as [a "solution" rather than a "problem"](/en/blog/addiction-is-solution-not-problem) — a way to temporarily escape anxiety, stress, loneliness, or boredom. When gambling serves that function, "I'm winning overall" becomes the ultimate justification for keeping it going.

And the more you repeat that justification, the more the debt grows and the addiction deepens. A vicious cycle where the exit keeps getting further away.

## Are There Really People Who Are "Up Overall"?

They're not nonexistent. In the world of horse racing and sports betting, there is a tiny handful of "pros" who maintain a positive balance through exhaustive analysis and emotion-free bankroll management.

But their lives look nothing like what you'd imagine. They pour enormous amounts of time into data analysis and absorb significant losses during cold streaks. They operate on an entirely different level from the average gambler, with deep knowledge of [the structural risks of sports betting](/en/blog/sports-betting). Thinking "I can win overall too" is like a weekend softball player watching the pros on TV and thinking, "I could do that."

And even if someone is technically in the black financially — what about the time spent, the sleep lost, the relationships strained? Can you really call that "winning" when you factor all of that in? When you think about it that way, the answer starts to shift.

## Breaking Free from the Spell of "Winning Overall"

### Write Down Every Single Transaction

Illusions thrive when you rely on memory. Use an app, a notebook — whatever works — and record everything down to the last yen. Include the transportation costs, the meals, the hours spent at the pachinko parlor. When you lay the numbers out side by side, you might notice the gap between the "wins" in your memory and what actually happened.

### Borrow Someone Else's Perspective

The tricky thing about cognitive bias is that it's nearly impossible to spot on your own. Show your records to someone you trust, or listen to the stories of people who've been through the same thing. Sharing your experience in a community like QuitMate can be one way to regain an objective point of view.

### Look at What's Really Behind the Gambling

Is it about making money? Killing boredom? Running from something? The real reason people turn to gambling is different for everyone. But just asking yourself the question can be enough to [start shifting something](/en/blog/reflection-changes-behavior).

## In Closing

"I'm winning overall." The next time those words come to mind, take just a moment to pause.

Confirmation bias. The gambler's fallacy. The sunk cost fallacy. The names might sound academic, but what they describe is simple: your brain writing a story that makes you feel good. This is a feature built into every human brain — there's nothing shameful about it.

But whether or not you can recognize the illusion makes a real difference in the choices that lie ahead.

Writing down your actual numbers might be a painful exercise. You might not like what the figures show. But it also means knowing exactly where you stand right now.

And if facing it alone feels like too much, it's okay to lean on people who've walked the same road.
